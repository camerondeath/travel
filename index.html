“Shanghai is not one city. It is four or five cities occupying the same geography at the same time, each from a different century, none willing to leave.”

You arrived past midnight after eighteen hours in the air. Before you try to organise anything today, it is worth sitting with a thought J. G. Ballard put into the novel you have in your bag. In Empire of the Sun, the boy Jim watches Shanghai from the window of a car moving through the International Settlement and cannot quite make sense of what he is seeing — too much, at too many scales, from too many eras, all operating simultaneously. That bewilderment is not a failure of comprehension. It is the correct response to Shanghai. The city does not resolve. It accretes. Today, jetlagged and new to it, you are in exactly the right state to receive it.

Walk north from the Pullman this morning and you reach the Bund in about fifteen minutes. The Bund (外滩) is the waterfront promenade that was, between roughly 1860 and 1943, the financial spine of Asia — HSBC, Standard Chartered, Jardine Matheson, the Hong Kong and Shanghai Bank, the Customs House, the Hongkong and Shanghai Hotels, the North China Daily News. Neo-Gothic, Baroque, Beaux-Arts, Art Deco, all lined up along the Huangpu River with the studied confidence of institutions that expected to be there permanently. They were wrong. After 1949, the buildings were nationalised or abandoned. The foreign firms were expelled. What remains is the skeleton of a financial empire that no longer exists, preserved because the buildings were too solid to demolish and too magnificent to ignore. The institutional names on the facades are, in most cases, now Chinese banks.

Ballard understood the Bund’s strange temporal quality. The Shanghai he wrote about — 1937–1945, Japanese occupation, internment, collapse — happened in and around these same streets. The Cathay Hotel (now the Fairmont Peace Hotel) was already a landmark when Jim’s parents dined there. The Bund’s neo-Gothic towers were the backdrop to a city unravelling. Walking the promenade this morning, you are walking the same stones. The buildings have been cleaned. The skyline behind them, in Pudong, did not exist when Ballard wrote the novel. But the river smell is the same. The proportions are the same. There is a continuity here that Shanghai’s rapid development has not quite managed to erase.

Things to notice on the Bund: The buildings face east, toward the river — toward money arriving by boat, which was the point. The facades were designed to be seen from the water, not from the promenade. Pudong, visible across the Huangpu, was farmland in 1990. The distance between the colonial banking hall on your left and the 101-storey Shanghai Tower on your right is about 650 metres and roughly a century of compressed development. Stand roughly at the Waldorf end (the southern stretch) for the cleanest composed view of both skylines simultaneously. Go back after dark — the Pudong towers are lit and the relationship between the two skylines is entirely different when the Bund is softly illuminated and Pudong is neon.

Rockbund Art Museum

Rockbund Art Museum at 20 Huqiu Lu was the Royal Asiatic Society — Shanghai’s intellectual club for British residents, built in 1932 in Art Deco with Romanesque elements, by the Hungarian architect László Hudec who designed some of the finest buildings of treaty port Shanghai. Today it is one of the better contemporary art institutions in mainland China, operating in a context where exhibition content remains subject to political constraints, and generally navigating them with some sophistication. The current exhibition, The Great Camouflage, runs until April 26.

Walking through Rockbund on your first morning matters for a reason beyond the show. This is the same neighbourhood where Sunday’s Monster Day Tours walk begins — Tiantong Lu, Huqiu Lu, the blocks immediately behind the Bund. Arriving here first means Sunday’s guide is contextualising streets you already know by feel. The building itself is the visit: the Art Deco interior, the curve of the main gallery, the proportion of the rooms. Spend an hour. Don’t rush it. You are still on Shanghai time, which moves at a pace that does not require hustle.

The Friday Muslim Market — your only chance

Today is a Friday, and that matters. After Friday prayers at Huxi Mosque on Changde Lu (1328 Changde Lu, Jing’an), a market appears outside that has nothing to do with the version of Shanghai the tourism industry presents. Vendors from Xinjiang — China’s far-western region, home to the Uyghur Muslim population — sell lamb kebabs over charcoal, rounds of nan from a tandoor, cold noodles dressed with cumin and chilli, dried fruits and nuts piled on folding tables. It runs for a few hours after prayers and then disappears. Twenty minutes by DiDi from the Pullman. This is the only Friday of your trip. If you have energy this afternoon, it is the correct use of it.

The market operates in a charged political context worth being honest about. Xinjiang has been the subject of sustained international criticism over China’s treatment of the Uyghur Muslim population — mass detention at scale, surveillance, cultural suppression, restrictions on religious practice. The vendors at this market are people going about their lives, selling food, in the same city as you. The food is genuinely extraordinary — the lamb is spiced correctly, the flatbread has the right char, the cumin noodles are unlike anything you have had. The situation is complicated. Both things are true simultaneously. You will form your own view walking through it.

Lunch: Chun or the alternatives

Chun (春) at 124 Jinxian Lu, French Concession, is one of the classic Shanghainese home-cooking restaurants — the kind of place that has been doing the same dishes for decades without particular interest in what the food media thinks of it. No reservations; arrive slightly before 1 PM. Lion’s head meatball (红烧狮子头), smoked fish, red-braised pork. The concierge has been asked to send someone to confirm timing. If jet lag has truly won, Huanghe Lu (黄河路) near People’s Square is a five-minute walk from the Bund: an old food street that became famous again through the TV drama Blossoms Shanghai. Point at the steamers. Eat whatever arrives.

On Shanghainese cuisine — what you are eating this week: Shanghai cooking (本帮菜, benbang cai) is defined by sweetness, soy, and slow-braising. The technique hong shao (红烧, “red-braising”) — soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, sugar, slow heat — is the foundation. Unlike Cantonese cooking (lightness, freshness) or Sichuan (heat, numbing spice), Shanghainese cuisine builds flavour through time. You will also encounter Shanghainese snacks: sheng jian bao (pan-fried pork dumplings, crispy on the bottom), xiao long bao (soup dumplings), and cong you bing (spring onion flatbread). All of them originated in this city or this region. All of them are worth eating whenever they appear.

The bigger picture you are stepping into

China is the second-largest economy on earth and is on a trajectory to become the first within the next decade or two. It is the world’s largest trading partner for most countries, including New Zealand, whose agricultural exports are substantially dependent on Chinese demand. The relationship between China’s rise and the US-led Western order is the defining geopolitical question of the next generation — more so now that the assumptions underpinning that order are under visible strain. You are here at an interesting moment: post-COVID reopening, a China that is methodically presenting itself to international visitors again, and a world that is still trying to decide what to make of it.

What you are watching this week is not just a city. It is a civilisation making a deliberate argument about what it is and where it is going. The Bund is part of that argument — the preserved colonial architecture as evidence of a historical arc that has now been reversed. Pudong is part of that argument — what thirty years of directed development can accomplish. The subway system, the electric vehicles, the cashless payment infrastructure, the surveillance cameras on every corner, the facial recognition at the train station — all part of the same argument. Whether you find it compelling, troubling, or simply extraordinary is worth examining each day. The most useful thing you can do this week is form a baseline impression before the question becomes inescapable from home.

From New Zealand’s specific position: China is simultaneously our largest trading partner and a source of genuine strategic uncertainty. Farmlands’ shareholders include people whose livelihoods are directly tied to Chinese commodity demand — dairy, beef, lamb. The question of how to engage, how to depend, how to hedge, is not settled policy anywhere in the world right now. You are here partly to see the place behind the abstraction. That is not a small thing.

The Ballard family lived comfortably — servants, a large garden, the social round of the British community. Jim is eleven. The city he lives in is already deeply strange: the Bund, the treaty port concessions, the Japanese-occupied Chinese districts surrounding them, the vast refugee camps outside the city walls. Xinhua Lu is where the domestic part of that story begins.

Qingming Festival

Today is Qingming Festival (清明节), one of the four major Chinese traditional festivals and a national public holiday. It is the festival of tomb-sweeping — families visit and clean ancestors’ graves, make offerings of food and paper goods. In parks you may see paper money and paper objects being burned: paper iPhones, paper cars, paper houses, paper credit cards — goods for use in the afterlife, transmitted through fire. This practice is around 2,500 years old. The specific objects change (paper smartphones are a relatively recent addition); the logic does not. It is not superstition in any dismissive sense. It is a serious engagement with the question of obligation to the dead, which Chinese culture has maintained across political revolutions, Maoist anti-traditionalism campaigns, and everything else.

The seasonal food to find today: qingtuan (青团) — green rice dumplings made with wormwood or mugwort paste, filled with red bean paste or black sesame. They exist for roughly six weeks a year, and this week is the heart of it. Street vendors have them; Shen Dacheng confectionery shops (several in the French Concession) always stock them in April. The colour is genuinely bright green — a vivid chlorophyll green from the plant matter incorporated into the rice. The taste is of grass and sweetness simultaneously. Worth going out of your way for, because in three weeks they will be gone for another year.

The Power Station of Art

The PSA is a former coal-fired power station — built in the late 1930s, it generated electricity for central Shanghai until the 1980s. The conversion in 2012 was deliberately minimal: the turbine hall remains a vertiginous void, the industrial bones are visible, the scale of the original function is legible. The current 15th Shanghai Biennale, “Does the Flower Hear the Bee?”, runs inside it — 250+ works from 67 international artists across multiple floors. Whether the show rewards the time is secondary to what the building itself provides: after a morning of intimate lane house scale — narrow courtyards, low ceilings, the compressed domesticity of longtang living — walking into the PSA atrium is a genuine spatial shock. The contrast is intentional. It is the whole shape of the day.

On the Shanghai Biennale: The Shanghai Biennale began in 1996 and is one of the oldest and most significant in Asia. It operates within constraints — political content requires navigation, certain topics are not viable — but it consistently shows ambitious international work. The 15th edition’s title references research on plant consciousness and interspecies communication. Whether this strikes you as profound or fashionably obscure will depend on your disposition. The building justifies the visit regardless. Allow 90 minutes minimum; the turbine hall alone takes time to absorb. Arrive before 3:30 PM — it closes at 5 PM and the upper floors require time.

The French Concession: what it actually was

The French Concession (法租界) existed from 1849 to 1943. It is worth being precise about what this means. It was not a French colony. French law nominally applied, but the residents were predominantly Chinese. The French municipal council collected rates, maintained the streets, planted the plane trees. The reason wealthy Chinese families chose to build here was legal security — the French framework provided better property protections than the Chinese-administered areas. This produced a specific housing type: the longtang (里弄), a hybrid that combined European street-grid organisation with Chinese courtyard domestic logic. The gate, the courtyard, the staircase, the family kitchen — European form, Chinese occupation. It was invented right here and exists nowhere else.

The Concession also sheltered people the authorities in other parts of the city wanted. Communist Party founding congress meetings were held in buildings you will walk past this week. Nationalist intelligence operated from villa compounds. Jewish refugees from Europe settled in adjacent Hongkou from 1938. The French Concession was, throughout its existence, a zone where the normal rules were slightly suspended — which is precisely why so much of Shanghai’s interesting history happened in it, and why Ballard’s family chose to live there.

A thread for the week: China and the rural question

You run a rural buying co-operative in New Zealand. Walking through the French Concession’s wet markets this morning, you are in the presence of something that has no direct equivalent in the NZ context: a food distribution system of genuine complexity serving 26 million people through a combination of centralised logistics, small-scale vendors, and informal networks that have evolved over centuries. The Chinese government’s relationship to food security is one of its central preoccupations — China has 20% of the world’s population and 7% of its arable land. The agricultural policy responses to this constraint — collective farming, the Green Revolution, market liberalisation, the current push toward agricultural technology — have shaped the country as much as any political ideology. The wet market you are walking through this morning is the downstream end of an agricultural system under enormous pressure. Worth noticing as a professional, not just as a tourist.

Ballard and the observer at the window

One of the recurring images in Empire of the Sun is Jim watching things from a distance he cannot close. He watches the Japanese Zero fighters from the roof of his house. He watches the adult world of the internment camp from its margins, never quite inside and never quite outside. He watches Shanghai from a car window in the opening pages, trying to make sense of a city that presents more information than any child can process. The observer-at-a-distance is Ballard’s defining mode — it is also, arguably, the only honest position for a foreigner arriving in Shanghai for the first time.

You are not going to understand China this week. That is not the point and it is not a failure. What you can do is observe carefully: the things that are unfamiliar (and why), the things that are surprisingly familiar (and why), the things that resist both categories. The International Settlement walk this morning is an exercise in exactly that kind of observation: a guide explaining a city to you from the outside, helping you build a framework, while you simultaneously form your own impressions. Both are happening at once. Both are valid. The difference between a good traveller and a bad one is whether they notice the gap between the framework and the impression, and stay curious about what lives in that gap.

1933 Shanghai — the afternoon destination

1933 Shanghai (1933老场坊) at 611 Liyang Lu, Hongkou, is one of the strangest buildings in China. It was designed in 1933 by a British architect (the exact attribution is contested, though the Balfour Group is most commonly cited) as a municipal abattoir — the facility that processed the city’s meat supply. The design problem was genuinely complex: how do you move large numbers of livestock in, process them efficiently, keep the animals as calm as possible, and move the product out, all within a single building? The solution was a building of extraordinary formal invention: a central cavernous atrium with concrete walkways spiralling upward around it, flying bridges connecting the levels at multiple heights, oculi letting light filter through the space, Baroque ornamental detailing applied to industrial surfaces. It is simultaneously beautiful and specific to its brutal purpose. The cows never cared. The building is extraordinary.

It was converted in 2006 into a creative and retail complex, but has never quite settled into being anything coherent. The conversion left the bones intact. Walking through it now feels slightly uncanny: the scale and the residual atmosphere of the original function are still present, but they are occupied by coffee shops and fashion studios and occasional art installations. This is not a criticism — it is precisely what makes it worth visiting. 1933 is what happens when a city is too architecturally serious to demolish its own past but too commercially driven to leave it empty. It is a building in an unresolved state, which is the most honest state for a building of this history to be in.

The Hongkou alternative: If you take the fork into deeper Hongkou rather than 1933, the Jewish Refugee Museum (Ohel Moishe Synagogue, Changyang Road) is the serious anchor. Between 1938 and 1941, approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees from Europe arrived in Shanghai — one of the very few cities in the world that did not require a visa for stateless persons. They lived in a designated area of Hongkou (the “Designated Area for Stateless Refugees”) that was essentially a ghetto, under conditions of genuine poverty, but they survived the war. The museum is carefully done and sombre. Zhapu Lu food street, five minutes away, is one of Shanghai’s original food streets — seafood and Shanghainese at pavement tables, almost entirely local. On Pudong and what it means to build a skyline in thirty years

Standing on the Bund and looking east at Pudong, hold two things simultaneously. The first: the skyline you are looking at is thirty years old. The Oriental Pearl Tower opened in 1994. The Jin Mao Tower in 1998. The Shanghai World Financial Center in 2008. The Shanghai Tower — now the second-tallest building in the world — in 2015. This is not gradual urban development. This is a civilisation that made a decision and executed it at a pace that has no precedent in the history of cities. Pudong was farmland in 1990. In 2025 it contains some of the most technically complex skyscrapers ever built. The question of what political and economic system makes this possible — and at what cost — is the right question to be sitting with.

The second thing: the people who built this were not abstractions. They were engineers and migrant workers from Anhui and Jiangsu who came to build the towers and lived in conditions you will not see from the Bund. China’s economic development over the past four decades is the largest reduction of absolute poverty in human history by most metrics — hundreds of millions of people moved from subsistence into something approaching material comfort. It is also a process that generated extraordinary inequality, displaced millions of people from their homes for development, destroyed enormous quantities of architectural and cultural heritage, and was directed by a one-party state with no tolerance for organised dissent. The honest position is to hold all of this simultaneously, without resolving it into either admiration or condemnation. Both are too easy, and both miss the actual complexity of what you are looking at.

From a New Zealand perspective: China’s trajectory over the next two decades will shape the market conditions for everything Farmlands’ shareholders produce. The question is not whether to engage with China but how — at what distance, on what terms, with what understanding of the risks. You are here partly to see the place behind the abstraction. The Pudong skyline is the most visible version of the argument China is making about itself. Worth spending time with, and worth being honest about your own response to it.

What the French Concession actually was

The Monster Day Tours walk at 3:30 PM (317 Ning Hai Dong Lu) will cover this in detail, but the historical skeleton is worth having before you arrive:

The French Concession (法租界) existed from 1849 to 1943, when the Vichy French government surrendered it to the Japanese-aligned Chinese government. During those ninety-four years, it was a zone administered by French municipal law, with a French council that collected rates, maintained public works, and planted the plane trees. But the residents were predominantly Chinese. The wealthy Shanghainese families who built their homes here were not French, did not speak French, and were not particularly interested in France. They were interested in the legal framework: property rights, enforcement of contracts, protection from the lawlessness and predation that characterised the Chinese-administered districts adjacent.

This produced the longtang (里弄, also called shikumen in their older form): a hybrid building type combining European street-grid organisation with Chinese courtyard domestic logic. A gate off the lane. A small courtyard. A three- or four-storey house with a kitchen on the ground floor, bedrooms above, a roof terrace. European form; Chinese occupation; invented in Shanghai; found nowhere else. The ones you have been walking past all week are the domestic architecture of a class that no longer exists — the compradors, the merchants, the professional families of the treaty port era. They are gradually disappearing, replaced by the apartment towers visible on every horizon.

The Concession also sheltered people the authorities elsewhere in the city wanted. The Communist Party held its founding congress in 1921 in a building at 76 Xingye Lu (now a museum, a ten-minute walk from here). Nationalist intelligence operated from villa compounds on the quieter streets. Sun Yat-sen lived here. Chiang Kai-shek’s wife’s family had property here. The French Concession was, throughout its existence, a zone where the normal rules were slightly suspended — which is why so much of China’s consequential twentieth-century history happened in it.

Those who do come for one of three things: the canal old town, the birthplace of Lu Xun, or the wine. You will do all three, in varying combinations, over the next two days.

The old town (古城区) is a genuine urban remnant of a pre-industrial Chinese city — not a reconstruction or a theme park, but a lived-in neighbourhood of stone-paved lanes, canal waterways, Qing and Ming-dynasty lane houses, and a pace that has nothing to do with Shanghai’s. The wupeng boat (乌篷船) — a low, narrow canal gondola with a distinctive black curved canopy, propelled by a boatman using his feet while lying back — is the specific image of Shaoxing that appears in every depiction of the city for the last two centuries. It is not a tourist confection; it is the traditional transport of the canal system, still operated by families who have been doing this for generations.

Cangqiao Zhijie (仓桥直街) is the canal street to walk tonight: stone-paved, Qing-dynasty lane houses on both sides, the canal running through the centre, best at dusk when the light softens and the canal reflects it. Walk its full length. No agenda. This is the correct speed for arriving in a canal town after five days in Shanghai. The city will do the rest.

On deceleration as intention

The entire structure of this trip was built around the transition happening today. Shanghai is the intensification: dense, stimulating, demanding. Shaoxing is the decompression: slow, specific, quiet. The instinct when arriving somewhere new is to immediately fill the time. Resist it. The best use of this evening is dinner near the canal, warm wine in a ceramic cup, stinky tofu, and early bed. Tomorrow is genuinely rich — Shen’s Garden, Lu Xun’s birthplace, the Xianheng lunch. Those things are better if you arrive at them rested rather than already exhausted. Tonight is not for optimising. It is for arriving.

There is a Ballard note worth ending on. In one of his late essays, he described Shanghai as “the city that dreams itself.” By which he meant: it has always been more than what it actually is; it generates its own mythology faster than any external observer can process it. Leaving it today, even temporarily, gives you the distance to see what you have been inside. The train journey is forty minutes of flat Yangtze Delta landscape — rice fields, canal networks, low housing. This is the country that feeds Shanghai. Worth watching out the window rather than at a screen. You are moving through the agricultural heartland of China’s most productive province. It looks, from the train window, unremarkable. It is not.

Shen’s Garden

Shen’s Garden (沈园) is a Song Dynasty garden, roughly 900 years old, that contains one of the more famous love stories in Chinese literary history. The poet Lu You (陆游, 1125–1210) married his cousin Tang Wan when both were young. His mother found the marriage unsuitable — Tang Wan was too independent, too intellectual, and Lu You was too preoccupied with her to focus on his official career — and forced the marriage to end. Both remarried others. Years later, by chance, they met in this garden. Tang Wan was there with her new husband; Lu You was alone. He wrote a poem on the garden wall and left. Tang Wan read it, wrote a response on the same wall, and died shortly after, reportedly from grief.

Both poems are called Chai Tou Feng (钗头凤, usually translated as “Phoenix Hairpin” or “The Hairpin”). They use the same rhyme scheme and respond to each other directly — a conversation in verse across a garden wall, between two people who were not supposed to be in love. The poems have been memorised by Chinese schoolchildren for eight hundred years. They appear regularly in Chinese popular culture — referenced in songs, dramatised repeatedly, quoted at moments of parting. They are the Chinese equivalent of a Romeo and Juliet allusion: shorthand for a specific kind of irreversible loss.

The garden is small and deliberately understated. It is not a grand imperial garden — no pavilions or artificial mountains, no long ceremonial vistas. It is a courtyard garden: enclosed, intimate, layered with the sense of something that happened here once and left a residue. Go early, before the tour groups arrive. The replica of the garden wall with the inscribed poems is there; the emotional weight of the original story is harder to feel with forty people photographing it. Go at 9:30 AM and you will often have most of it to yourself.

Lu Xun, in full

Lu Xun (鲁迅, born Zhou Shuren, 1881–1936) is the most important writer in modern Chinese literature, and one of the most important writers of the twentieth century by any serious standard. He grew up in Shaoxing in a family of declining scholar-gentry status — the family wealth dissipated, his father chronically ill, the household slowly contracting. He went to Japan to study medicine, intending to save Chinese people from physical disease. While studying in Sendai, he saw a photograph: a Chinese man about to be executed by the Japanese, surrounded by a crowd of Chinese onlookers who were watching with what he described as expressions of blank numbness. He decided in that moment that China’s problem was not physical disease but something more intractable — a cultural and spiritual passivity that made atrocity possible because people had learned not to resist it. He quit medicine and became a writer.

His most famous works: “A Madman’s Diary” (1918) — the first major piece of Chinese fiction written in the vernacular rather than classical Chinese, in which a narrator experiencing paranoid delusions reads Chinese history as a record of “cannibalism”; “The True Story of Ah Q” (1921) — a savage portrait of a village fool who constructs psychological victories from his defeats, which Lu Xun intended as an indictment of a specifically Chinese mode of self-deception; and “Kong Yiji” (1919), the story of a failed scholar who drinks at the Xianheng tavern — the restaurant where you are eating lunch — and whose humiliation the narrator watches with the same blank numbness Lu Xun was trying to diagnose in the whole culture.

The paradox of Lu Xun’s official status: Lu Xun is simultaneously one of the most radical and subversive writers in Chinese literary history — his work is fundamentally about what is wrong with Chinese society and culture — and the officially sanctioned national literary hero. Mao Zedong called him “the saint of modern China.” His work is mandatory in the school curriculum. There are statues of him everywhere. The Communist Party both claims him and cannot fully contain what he wrote. This is the kind of contradiction that China does very well: incorporating its critics into the national mythology by reframing what they were criticising. Worth thinking about.

The birthplace complex

The Lu Xun Birthplace Complex (鲁迅故里) encompasses his childhood home, the private school where he studied (the Three Flavours Study, 三味书屋), the surrounding lane-house neighbourhood preserved at an intimate scale, and the Xianheng tavern. The childhood home is a large gentry house — the contrast between its relative comfort and the family’s declining fortunes is part of what you understand walking through it. The Three Flavours Study is the private school where Lu Xun’s teacher, Mr Shou Jingwu, ran a rigorous Confucian education. Lu Xun both revered and resisted him. The school is tiny and specific: a courtyard, a teaching room, the desks, the smell of old wood. Come here before the Xianheng lunch and the meal will have a different quality.

Lunch at the Xianheng

The Xianheng Hotel (咸亨酒店) appears in “Kong Yiji” as the tavern where the story’s narrator watches Kong Yiji — the failed scholar — drink, beg, be mocked, and eventually disappear. The current Xianheng is a reconstruction on the original site: tourist-oriented in the sense that it knows precisely why you are there, but genuinely serving the dishes from the stories. Order the fennel broad beans (茴香豆) — the specific dish Kong Yiji counts out carefully when buying wine. They taste of anise and slight bitterness, eaten as a drinking snack with warm Huadiao wine. Order them even if you are uncertain about fennel. Context is part of taste, and eating the dish from the story in the place from the story is an experience with no substitute.

Also order: braised pork belly in Shaoxing wine (绍兴焖肉), stinky tofu (臭豆腐) if you are willing, and the warm Huadiao wine in ceramic cups. The food is good. The setting is the point. You are eating inside a literary context, which is rarer than eating in a good restaurant.

The capacity to take in new information is not unlimited and does not benefit from being pushed past its natural limit. A wupeng boat is genuinely worth doing in Shaoxing; so is the Orchid Pavilion with its 353 AD calligraphy context. Both will still exist on a second visit. The wetland park outside your hotel door is the point of having chosen that hotel over something in the old town. Use it. Sit by the water. Read the book you have been carrying. This is not settling for less. This is the correct version of the afternoon.

On the wupeng boat (乌篷船): If you have genuine energy after lunch, the wupeng boat is the better choice than the wetland park. The black-canopied canal gondola — propelled by the boatman using his feet against the oar handles while lying back at an angle — is a boat design unique to this region. A 40-minute trip punts you through the old town’s canal system at water level: the view from the canal is entirely different from the view from the street, lower and slower and more intimate. The dock is near Shenliquan. Walk there from the Xianheng — fifteen minutes on foot along the canal. Decide when you are standing at the water rather than planning in advance.
