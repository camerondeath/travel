The River Before You Know It

Opening

You will arrive at this city having read about it, or imagined it, or absorbed it through the accumulated residue of a thousand references. None of that preparation will quite work. Shanghai operates below the level of advance understanding. It is a city that insists on being encountered directly, and it takes the first day or two before it begins to settle into something you can read.

Today is not for ambition. You arrived past midnight with a redeye behind you and a week ahead. The Bund will be fifteen minutes on foot. Rockbund is directly behind it. The Friday Muslim market on Changde Lu exists for one afternoon and then disappears for another week. The city after dark is a different city from the one under morning cloud. None of this requires rushing.

“Shanghai is not a city that explains itself. It rewards the people who stay long enough to notice.”— Historian Jeffrey Wasserstrom
The Bund — What You Are Looking At

The strip of colonial buildings along the western bank of the Huangpu was never meant to be beautiful. It was meant to be legible — a statement of commercial and imperial power written in stone at the edge of a treaty port city that China had been forced to open at gunpoint. The first Opium War ended in 1842; Shanghai became one of five ports compelled to admit foreign traders. What followed was one of history’s stranger experiments: a city partitioned into concessions — British, French, American, later Japanese — each operating under its own laws and courts, a sovereign patchwork jammed against the Chinese city surrounding it.

The Bund’s centrepiece — the HSBC headquarters (now Shanghai Pudong Development Bank), the domed neoclassical building — was completed in 1923 and was briefly the second-largest bank building in the world. The Customs House beside it dates to 1927; its clock tower still rings the hour, though the melody was changed to “The East is Red” during the Cultural Revolution and only restored to Westminster chimes in 1997. The Cathay Hotel — now the Peace Hotel — opened in 1929 under Victor Sassoon, the Iraqi Jewish magnate who built much of the Bund. Charlie Chaplin stayed there. Noël Coward wrote Private Lives there, delirious with influenza.

After 1949, the buildings were nationalised. They deteriorated quietly. The Cultural Revolution brought slogans painted across stonework. But the bones of the treaty port remained — tearing them down would have cost more than leaving them standing.

The Pudong Decision

In 1990, Deng Xiaoping approved the Pudong development zone directly opposite the Bund. The Oriental Pearl Tower broke ground in 1991. By the 2010 World Expo, Pudong had a skyline of declared ambition. The symbolism is deliberate: the past on the west bank, the future on the east, the river between them. The Bund tonight is where that argument is most legible. Walk south toward the Waldorf end as the lights come up. That ten-minute window when both banks are in amber suspension, before the LED towers assert themselves, is the real first viewing.

Rockbund — The Building Before the Art

The Royal Asiatic Society building at 20 Huqiu Lu was completed in 1932. The architect was Hungarian-born László Hudec, who arrived in Shanghai in 1918 as a prisoner of war escaping from a Trans-Siberian train and stayed for twenty-seven years, designing some of the most significant buildings in the city. The RAS branch housed a library, a natural history museum, and a lecture hall where serious papers were read on Chinese archaeology — often by the same men who profited from the trade arrangements that kept the treaty port running.

The current show, The Great Camouflage, takes its title from the Martinican poet Suzànne Césaire, who used the phrase to describe how colonised cultures survived by appearing to adapt while preserving interior resistance. A show about camouflage and colonial resistance, in a building purpose-built for the cultural arm of a colonial power. The location makes the frame uncomfortable in productive ways. Stand in the lobby first. Look up at the staircase. The building is worth seeing before the art.

The Friday Market — The Uyghur Question

Outside Huxi Mosque on Changde Lu on Friday afternoons, Xinjiang vendors set up charcoal grills and sell lamb kebabs, flatbread from a tandoor, dried fruit from Kashgar. It is one of the most politically weighted food experiences you will have on this trip.

The Uyghurs are a Turkic Muslim people, around twelve million of whom live in Xinjiang. Since approximately 2017, the Chinese government has operated a system of mass detention, surveillance, and forced cultural assimilation that human rights organisations and leaked internal documents describe as the largest internment of an ethnic minority since the Second World War. The Chinese government calls these “vocational training centres.” The satellite imagery, survivor testimony, and the leaked Xinjiang Papers suggest something closer to a network of forced detention camps.

The Uyghur community in Shanghai predates these events by decades — traders following the old Silk Road logic toward China’s commercial capital. Their presence is tolerated in ways not possible in Xinjiang itself, where mosque architecture has been forcibly modified, Arabic script removed from public signage, and religious practice restricted. The Friday market exists in a kind of tacit permission: visible enough to be known, small enough not to require a response.

What you are eating is genuinely good food. It is also the food of a people under sustained political pressure, sold openly in a city whose government is conducting that pressure. Both things are true simultaneously. The vendors are not symbols; they are people selling lunch. But context changes what you taste.

“To eat is a necessity, but to eat intelligently is an art.”— La Rochefoucauld, which the French brought to Shanghai, and which Shanghai promptly improved upon
Shanghai in 2026

Shanghai is the most cosmopolitan city in China and one of the most surveilled. It holds both conditions without apparent contradiction. The cameras are everywhere — on every major intersection, reading faces and plates — and so are the restaurants, galleries, jazz bars, and independent bookshops. The city has always managed this balance: open enough to be useful to the world, controlled enough to remain useful to the Party.

The two-month lockdown of spring 2022 — twenty-five million people confined, many to government quarantine facilities — left something behind: a residue of distrust, a sharpened awareness of what the state could and would do. The outward mood has returned to normal. Underneath, there is less optimism than 2019. The property crisis hollowed out middle-class wealth. Youth unemployment hit record highs. Xi Jinping’s consolidation of power is essentially complete — no term limits, no visible successor, no institutional check.

For a visitor, this is mostly invisible. Shanghai will feel vital and open. What you will occasionally notice is what is not there: the searches that return nothing, the topics that produce a careful subject change, the generation who have concluded the best strategy is not protest but elegant withdrawal — working less, consuming selectively, refusing the logic of relentless ambition. They call it tang ping: lying flat.

The French Concession — What You Are Walking Into

The French Concession was never really French. It was a piece of Shanghai that France controlled — its own legal system, police force, tax regime — from 1849 until 1943, when Vichy France, already occupied by Germany, voluntarily surrendered the concession to Japan’s Chinese puppet government. For nearly a century, an area roughly three kilometres square operated as a state within a state, answerable to Paris rather than Beijing.

The French planted plane trees — Platanus acerifolia, now so old their roots have cracked the pavements — along every major boulevard. They built villas, apartments, a cathedral, a racecourse, a prison. The more interesting residents weren’t French at all. Zhou Enlai organised Communist cells here in the 1920s, choosing it precisely because French jurisdiction made it harder for Nationalist police to operate. Chiang Kai-shek lived here. The revolutionary politics of this neighbourhood are worth sitting with: the Chinese Communist Party was incubated in apartments on streets administered by a European colonial power, protected from Chinese state violence by the very imperialism the party was organising against. History is not tidy.

“Shanghai has always been a place where people come to remake themselves. The question is who gets to decide what the new self looks like.”— Historian Jeffrey Wasserstrom
The Wet Market — What It Tells You

Shanghai has been systematically closing wet markets since the 1990s, replacing them with supermarkets and regulated food halls. The ones that remain operate under a kind of administrative tolerance that can be withdrawn. What happens at 8 AM is something between commerce, theatre, and genuine community — vendors who have occupied the same stalls for decades, prices negotiated not fixed, produce arriving fresh daily. The fish are alive. The tofu is made that morning.

The wet market is an informal economy within a formally planned state. The Party has periodically tried to eliminate it, citing hygiene and modernisation. It persists. The friction between top-down rationalisation and the granular stubbornness of how people actually live is one of the defining tensions of contemporary China — and you’ll see it in microcosm in a market lane at 8:30 on a Saturday morning.

Qingming — The Festival Under Your Feet

Today is the first day of Qingming — 清明, Clear and Bright — a three-day public holiday about the dead. Specifically, about your obligation to visit, clean, and honour the graves of ancestors, to burn paper offerings, to leave food. The festival is several thousand years old and survived the Cultural Revolution’s attempt to suppress ancestral veneration as feudal superstition.

In Shanghai, many families’ ancestors are buried in the countryside their grandparents came from. What survives most visibly in the city is the food: qingtuan — small green dumplings made from glutinous rice mixed with wormwood juice, filled with sweet red bean paste. They appear only in the weeks around Qingming and nowhere else. Slightly bitter from the greens, yielding, quietly sweet. They taste of the season. If you see them at a street vendor today, buy one.

The Power Station of Art

The PSA sits on the south Huangpu waterfront in a building that generated electricity for the city from 1897 until 1985. The conversion happened in 2012, and it is now the largest contemporary art museum in China. The 15th Shanghai Biennale — Does the Flower Hear the Bee? — raises, in the oblique language that international contemporary art uses to discuss things it cannot say directly, questions about ecology and the relationship between natural and human systems. The title is a genuine scientific question: recent research suggests plants may respond to the vibration of bee wings by increasing their nectar’s sugar content.

The Biennale is one of the few venues in China where international contemporary art — including work that touches on themes the government finds uncomfortable — is regularly displayed. The curators work within constraints Western curators don’t face. But the building alone justifies the trip. Stand in the atrium of the former turbine hall and look up. The scale of what it took to power a city of millions is still in the space.

“Art is not a mirror held up to reality but a hammer with which to shape it.”— Bertolt Brecht, whose exile briefly included Shanghai in the 1930s

The Layers Underneath
Sunday 5 April · Day Three
▼
Opening

Sunday in Shanghai. The weekend crowds will be out on Wukang Lu and the main Bund promenade, which is part of why today’s architecture walk begins early, in Hongkou, in a neighbourhood the tourist infrastructure hasn’t reached. By the time you’ve had lunch you will have walked through the remains of two of the most extraordinary communities ever to occupy a single square kilometre: the foreign merchants of the International Settlement, and the twenty thousand Jewish refugees who arrived from Europe between 1933 and 1941 and had nowhere else to go.

Both communities are gone. What remains is architecture, a museum, and questions about what it means when a city becomes someone else’s only option.

The International Settlement — Colonialism with Better Accounting

The British, Americans, and eventually Japanese had the International Settlement — larger than the French Concession, governed by its own Municipal Council, its own courts, its own police force (which included White Russian, Sikh, Chinese, and British officers — a multicultural institution of remarkable complexity). It ran from 1863 until 1941, when Japan’s attack on Pearl Harbor brought the Allied powers into the war and the Japanese military simply walked in.

The architecture you walk through today — the Art Deco facades of Tiantong Lu — is what the Settlement built at its peak. Not grand civic buildings, but the bones of a working commercial city: banks, offices, the apartment buildings where clerks lived. The proportions are right. This is what your guide will put names and histories to.

One thing worth knowing: the Settlement excluded the Chinese. The Municipal Council was elected by property-owning ratepayers — in practice, foreign nationals. Chinese residents, who outnumbered foreigners twenty to one, had no vote. The parks were, until 1928, explicitly closed to Chinese people. The apocryphal “No Dogs or Chinese” sign may or may not have existed verbatim, but the reality it describes was real. Both things are true: the buildings are beautiful, and they were built on extraordinary injustice.

“The foreigner in China always runs the risk of loving what was built on someone else’s exclusion.”— From a private letter, Shanghai, 1930s
The Jews of Shanghai — 20,000 People With Nowhere Else to Go

Between 1933 and 1941, approximately twenty thousand Jewish refugees fled to Shanghai. They came from Germany, Austria, Poland, occupied Europe. They came because Shanghai was one of the only places on earth that required no visa, no affidavit, no sponsor, no immigration quota. Britain enforced restrictions in Hong Kong. America had strict European quotas. Palestine was under British Mandate with its own entry controls. Australia required a deposit of £200. Shanghai simply required a ticket.

This was not idealism. Shanghai’s open-door status was an artifact of its treaty port structure — no single power fully controlled immigration, so nobody systematically enforced it. The result was accidental rescue on a massive scale.

The refugees settled primarily in Hongkou, under Japanese military control by then. In 1943, under pressure from Nazi Germany, the Japanese ordered all “stateless refugees” who had arrived since 1937 to relocate to a designated area of Hongkou — effectively a ghetto, though the Japanese never used that word. Around eighteen thousand people in roughly one square mile. They published newspapers, ran cabarets, opened restaurants, performed opera, established schools. They called their neighbourhood “Little Vienna.”

The Ohel Moishe Synagogue on Changyang Road holds photographs, documents, and survivor testimony. The story has a specific contemporary resonance the museum handles carefully: the country that provided refuge to Jews fleeing persecution is, in another part of itself, operating what UN experts describe as “serious human rights violations” against a different minority. History rhymes in uncomfortable ways.

1933 Shanghai — The Abattoir

The 1933 building at 611 Liyang Lu was completed in 1933 as the Shanghai Municipal Abattoir. Concrete walkways spiral in helical ramps around a four-storey central void, allowing cattle to be walked to slaughter at height without climbing stairs. Baroque ornamental detailing was applied to an industrial structure — fluted pilasters on a killing floor, decorative friezes on a building whose function was precisely the opposite of decorative.

Now a mixed-use creative complex. Stand at the centre of the void and look at the concrete spiralling around you. Try to hold both what the building was designed to do and what it is doing now. The gap between the two is the building’s real content.

“We came with nothing. Shanghai gave us the one thing we needed: the right to exist.”— Ernest Heppner, Shanghai Refuge: A Memoir of the World War II Jewish Ghetto (1993)

On the Monday Rhythm of Chinese Cities

The five-day work week was introduced in China in 1994, explicitly to stimulate domestic consumption: if workers have weekends, they will spend money on leisure. The weekday/weekend distinction in Chinese cities since then has been marked — weekend crowds in parks and on the Bund are dense; Monday has a perceptible decompression.

But China has been arguing with the working week again. In 2021, the government cracked down on the tech industry’s 996 culture — 9 AM to 9 PM, six days a week — declaring it illegal. The crackdown was partly genuine enforcement and partly a response to a generation who had begun openly rejecting the logic of overwork. The tang ping movement emerged simultaneously: a decision by educated young people to work only enough to survive, to refuse the ambition-and-consumption logic of their parents’ generation.

The government is alarmed by this, primarily because China’s demographic projections without a birth rate reversal are very bad. Various incentives are being tried. They are not working particularly well. The generation that was supposed to have children and buy apartments is lying flat instead.

The Yangtze Delta — What You Are Passing Through

The train crosses the southern Yangtze River Delta — one of the most historically productive agricultural and commercial regions on earth. The delta was the economic heartland of imperial China for more than a thousand years: rice surplus that fed the empire, silk production that drove the luxury trade, canal networks that connected the interior to the coast before railways existed.

The Grand Canal — begun in 486 BC and extended through dynasties until the 13th century — runs through this landscape. It connected Beijing to Hangzhou, nearly 1,800 kilometres, for the purpose of transporting southern grain to the northern capital. It was the longest man-made waterway in the world for most of human history. It is still in use. You will be passing above parts of it. The flat landscape, the canal grids, the low bridges — this is one of the oldest continuously inhabited and managed agricultural landscapes on the planet.

The High-Speed Railway — China’s Largest Infrastructure Argument

China has approximately 45,000 kilometres of high-speed rail — more than the rest of the world combined. The network barely existed in 2008; it now carries more passenger-kilometres annually than all of China’s domestic aviation. The G-class trains travel at 300+ km/h on tracks built to tolerances Western rail networks have not attempted at this scale.

Western analysis of Chinese infrastructure tends toward two poles: dismissal (it’s debt-fuelled, built to showcase) or alarm (it’s an unprecedented achievement demonstrating the superiority of state-directed development). Neither is adequate. The infrastructure is real and works. The debt is also real and a genuine long-term problem. The question of whether democratic systems can build infrastructure at this scale and speed — and if not, what the trade-offs of the alternatives are — is one of the animating political questions of the 2020s. You are on a train that is an argument. Look out the window.

“China’s great cities make noise about the future. Its smaller cities carry the weight of the past. Both are worth your attention, but they require different ears.”— Jonathan Spence, The Search for Modern China (1990)

Lu Xun — Why He Matters

Zhou Shuren, who wrote under the pen name Lu Xun (鲁迅), was born in Shaoxing in 1881 and died in Shanghai in 1936. He is, without much argument, the most important Chinese writer of the twentieth century. Not primarily a novelist — his fiction is relatively small in volume. He was an essayist, a satirist, a translator, and a diagnostician of exactly what was wrong with China at the moment it was trying to figure out what to become.

His “Diary of a Madman” (1918) is the first major Chinese fiction written in vernacular Mandarin rather than classical Chinese — a formal revolution. The story is told from the perspective of a man who has come to believe that Confucian social obligations are a system for eating people, literally and metaphorically. The “madman” is the only person who can see clearly; the sane people are the ones engaged in something monstrous.

“The True Story of Ah Q” (1921) concerns a peasant man whose psychological mechanism is to convert every defeat into a private victory. He is bullied, humiliated, and eventually executed for a crime he didn’t commit, finding at each stage a way to believe he has won. Lu Xun called this “spiritual victory” — a savage portrait of a self-deception he argued was characteristic of Chinese national psychology at the time: a society that had been losing to foreign powers for a century and developed elaborate internal narratives for why each loss was not actually a defeat.

Lu Xun remains politically charged because both the Communist Party and its critics want to claim him. Mao called him “the chief commander of China’s cultural revolution” (meaning the literary movement). But Lu Xun’s actual writing is deeply sceptical of ideology, suspicious of any collective solution to human suffering — and would almost certainly have put him in trouble had he lived past 1936.

“Hope cannot be said to exist, nor can it be said not to exist. It is just like roads across the earth. For actually the earth had no roads to begin with, but when many people pass one way, a road is made.”— Lu Xun, Hometown (故乡, 1921)
Shen’s Garden — The Poem on the Wall

Shen’s Garden (沈园) is a Song Dynasty garden that would be beautiful without its story. With its story, it is one of those places where a specific human moment has soaked into the stone and water and will not leave.

The poet Lu You (1125–1210) married his cousin Tang Wan, with whom he was genuinely in love, in 1144. His mother forced him to divorce her — officially incompatibility of horoscopes, probably because Tang Wan was considered a distraction from Lu You’s studies. Both subsequently remarried others. Years later, they encountered each other at Shen’s Garden. Tang Wan was there with her new husband; Lu You was there alone. Her husband offered Lu You wine — a gesture of unusual social grace. The encounter lasted briefly. Afterwards, Lu You wrote a poem on the garden wall: Chai Tou Feng (“Hairpin Phoenix”). Tang Wan wrote a response — her only surviving poem — on another wall. Both poems survive. Tang Wan died shortly after. Lu You lived to eighty-five, writing about the encounter and about her until the end of his life.

This is not a legend. It is a documented historical incident with surviving contemporary sources. The garden carries this weight quietly. Go before the tour groups arrive — before 9:30 AM — and sit by the water with it.

Xianheng — The Meal About Place

The Xianheng Hotel (咸亨酒店) on Lu Xun Road is a tavern, rebuilt on the original site, that appears in several of Lu Xun’s most famous stories. The original was a real establishment where people from all social strata drank together — or rather, did not drink together, the social separation visible in whether you stood at the counter (poor) or sat at a table (better off). Kong Yiji, one of Lu Xun’s most affecting stories, is set here: a failed scholar who drinks at the counter, recites classical allusions to people who don’t understand them, and is gradually destroyed by a society that has no use for the kind of knowledge he has.

Order the fennel broad beans (茴香豆, huíxi&amacute;ng dòu) — literally the dish from the story. Kong Yiji argues with the bartender about the number of ways the character for “fennel” can be written. They taste of the story more than of themselves. The braised pork belly in Shaoxing wine is the other anchor. Drink the Huadiao warm. You are in the city it was made in.

The speed of the train from Shanghai to Shaoxing and the surveillance cameras at every intersection are both expressions of the same state capacity.

As for what role China plays in ten or twenty years — the honest answer, which most analysts are too invested in a conclusion to say plainly, is: nobody knows. The country is too large, too internally diverse, too capable of surprising both itself and its observers. What you can observe, from this week’s vantage, is that it is not the monolith its critics describe, nor the rising juggernaut its boosters project. It is a country of 1.4 billion people in the middle of figuring out what it is. That process is considerably more interesting than either the triumphalist or the declinist narrative gives it credit for.

Things to Notice Today
  • At Shen’s Garden: the scale. It is small. The weight of what happened here is inversely proportional to the size of the space.
  • At Lu Xun’s birthplace: the Three Flavors Study. “To study is sweetness. To think is bitterness. To discuss is one of the flavours of neither.” This is the frame in which Lu Xun’s critical intelligence was formed.
  • At Xianheng: the other diners. This is a lunch place that happens to be historically significant, and the people eating beside you are treating it accordingly.
  • On the canal or in the bamboo or walking back through the afternoon: you are about to get on a plane back to New Zealand. Notice what it feels like to be about to leave a place. That feeling is data.
