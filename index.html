“Shanghai is the city China shows the world. The question worth carrying all week is what it shows itself.”

There is a passage early in Empire of the Sun — you are perhaps fifty pages in — where eleven-year-old Jim, newly separated from his parents in the chaos of the Japanese occupation, walks back through the deserted International Settlement. The Packards are gone. The cocktail parties have stopped. The White Russian doormen have evaporated. The city that was stage-set for his parents’ social calendar has simply revealed itself as something else: real, vast, indifferent to the concerns of the people who thought they owned it. What Ballard captures is that Jim is not frightened. He is, for the first time, paying attention. The city has been there all along. He has just, until now, been looking past it.

You will have a version of this experience this morning, minus the occupation. You arrived after midnight into a city of 26 million people and you have not yet seen any of it. The Bund is fifteen minutes by DiDi. Go.

How a fishing village became the most interesting city in the world

The compressed version, because you need it to make sense of everything you’ll see this week: Shanghai was a minor cotton-trading port at the mouth of the Yangtze Delta until 1842, when Britain concluded the First Opium War and forced China to open five coastal cities to foreign commerce. The Opium Wars are worth understanding because they are the foundational grievance that underpins almost everything China does geopolitically today, and because their logic is so nakedly cynical it still has the power to startle. Britain had a trade deficit with China — buying enormous quantities of tea, porcelain, and silk — and couldn’t find anything Chinese consumers wanted in exchange. A British trader in Calcutta solved the problem by flooding China with Indian opium. When the Chinese government seized and destroyed twenty thousand chests of it in 1839, Britain went to war to protect the right to keep selling drugs to Chinese people. Won. Extracted trading rights, reparations, and the principle — enshrined in what China still calls the “unequal treaties” — that foreigners in China would be governed by their own laws, not Chinese ones.

That last clause built Shanghai. The foreign concessions that took shape over the following decades were self-governing zones in the middle of a sovereign country. The International Settlement — British and American, later joined by most of the world — covered the Bund and the commercial grid behind it. The French had their own concession to the south, where they planted plane trees and built a residential neighbourhood that still has a faintly Mediterranean quality. In these concessions, Chinese law did not apply. Foreign courts heard foreign cases. The arrangement was called “extraterritoriality” and it was, by any honest accounting, a form of occupation conducted with paperwork rather than garrisons.

What happened inside that arrangement was extraordinary. Between 1842 and 1941, Shanghai became one of the five most important cities on earth. The most sophisticated financial centre in Asia. White Russians arrived in waves after 1917, bringing their restaurants and their violins and their particular gift for melancholy. Sephardic Jewish traders — the Sassoons, the Kadoories — built the great Art Deco towers on the Bund with fortunes made in the opium trade and diversified into cotton, property, and hotels. German and Austrian Jews fleeing the Nazi persecution found that Shanghai was one of the only cities on earth that required no visa, no sponsor, no proof of anything, and twenty thousand of them arrived between 1933 and 1941, settling in the Hongkou district north of Suzhou Creek, opening cafÃ©s and delicatessens and small orchestras. The Chinese Communist Party held its first congress in 1921 in a shikumen house in what is now Xintiandi — you’ll walk past the site tonight. The Green Gang ran opium distribution, protection rackets, and taxi-dance halls. When Chiang Kai-shek wanted the Communists purged in 1927, he contracted the work to the Green Gang, and a thousand people died in a week. NoÃ«l Coward finished Private Lives at the Cathay Hotel in 1930 while recovering from flu. Ballard’s family lived in Amherst Avenue in the French Concession, attended cocktail parties on Saturday nights, and drove past beggars every morning.

Then 1949 On May 25th, the People’s Liberation Army marched into Shanghai. Within months, every foreign business, every foreign resident, and every institution that had defined the city’s cosmopolitan character was gone. The jazz clubs closed. The racetrack became a public park — it’s still there, called People’s Square, with the Shanghai Museum sitting roughly where the finish line used to be. For thirty years, Shanghai was a city that had been switched off. Not destroyed — the buildings survived — but dormant. Then 1990: Deng Xiaoping designates Pudong a Special Economic Zone and switches it back on. What you see across the river this morning is thirty-five years of that decision running at full speed.

The two waterfronts

Stand on the Bund with your back to the river and look west. Thirty-two buildings, mostly between 1900 and 1937, built to house the commercial infrastructure of Western capitalism in Asia: the Hong Kong and Shanghai Bank, the Customs House (whose clock tower has kept time since 1927), the Fairmont Peace Hotel where Wallis Simpson and Charlie Chaplin both stayed. These buildings were designed to communicate permanence and the invincibility of Western capital in Asia. The people who built them expected to be here forever. They were gone within a generation. Now turn around. The skyline across the Huangpu — the corkscrew of the Pearl Tower, the tapered glass of the Shanghai Tower (the world’s second-tallest building), the bottle-opener silhouette of the World Financial Centre — is thirty-five years old. Pudong was rice paddies in 1990. The Chinese state built a rival skyline in a generation, deliberately, as a statement. The message is not subtle: we can do this too, faster, without asking anyone’s permission, and we didn’t have to sell anyone drugs to fund it. The two waterfronts are in permanent pointed dialogue across the Huangpu. No guide required to feel the argument.

Rockbund and what it tells you

The building you’ll visit this morning — the Rockbund Art Museum at 20 Huqiu Lu — is the former Royal Asiatic Society, completed in 1932. The RAS was the cultural institution the International Settlement built to give its merchant population a sense of intellectual credibility: lectures, a natural history collection, a library, the pretence of scholarship. Stand in the entry hall and notice the quality of light and proportion. These rooms were built for men in suits who expected to be here for centuries. The building has outlasted them, the settlement, and three political systems, and is now showing contemporary Chinese art. That particular irony — the colonial infrastructure repurposed by the culture it was designed to manage — is worth sitting with. It is also, incidentally, where Sunday’s Monster Day Tours walk begins. Come here first and the streets will feel pre-loaded with context when the guide arrives.

This evening: why Xintiandi is more interesting than it looks

Xintiandi is ten minutes on foot from the Pullman. Before you arrive this evening, it’s worth understanding what you’re walking into. In the mid-1990s, the Hong Kong developer Shui On Group acquired twenty-three blocks of dense, deteriorating shikumen housing in the old French Concession. The standard approach in 1990s Shanghai was demolition — the economics of land in central Shanghai, the government’s desire for modern infrastructure, the perceived backwardness of pre-Liberation housing stock. Shui On made a different argument: that the stone facades, the lane networks, the architectural texture of the neighbourhood could be preserved and given commercial purpose. The government agreed. What opened in 2001 was the first major historic preservation development in modern China, and a template that has since been replicated across the country.

The shikumen gates are original. The grey brick, the heavy timber doors, the characteristic arched stone frames that give the buildings their name — all real, all restored. The interiors have been gutted and replaced with restaurants, boutiques, and bars. The residents were relocated. The people who come now are the Chinese upper-middle class and international visitors in roughly equal measure, which is its own statement about how Shanghai has moved. At night, lit by low lanterns, with the plane trees overhead and the crowd doing its evening drift through the lanes, it is genuinely beautiful. It is also, one remove back, a kind of museum to a way of living that the modernisation of Shanghai has almost entirely erased. Every city makes choices about what to preserve and what to demolish. Xintiandi is Shanghai’s most deliberate one.

Qingming and what it means that China still does this

Today is Qingming Festival (æ¸æè) — Tomb-Sweeping Day — one of China’s major public holidays and one of its most ancient. For 2,500 years, Chinese families have spent this day visiting the graves of their ancestors: cleaning the stones, leaving food and flowers, burning paper offerings — paper money, paper iPhones, paper houses — to send goods to the dead through smoke. The practice was suppressed during the Cultural Revolution and reinstated as an official public holiday in 2008. That reinstatement is telling. The Communist Party, which spent a significant portion of its first three decades trying to break the back of traditional Chinese culture, eventually recognised that ancestor veneration was too deeply embedded in Chinese identity to abolish. You can ban the practice; you cannot remove the instinct.

What Qingming shows you is something important about the relationship between Chinese identity and time. In Western secular culture, the dead recede into the past. In Chinese tradition, the relationship between the living and the dead is continuous and obligatory: you owe your ancestors care, and the failure to provide it has consequences. This is not superstition in the dismissive sense — it is a coherent moral framework for understanding how individuals relate to the chain of people who produced them. It survived Marxist materialism, the Cultural Revolution, and forty years of urbanisation because it answers a question that ideology cannot. Walking past a park today and seeing families gathered with incense and paper offerings: that is 2,500 years of practice still in operation. Worth noticing.

The Power Station of Art and what it used to be

The afternoon move to the Power Station of Art is a deliberate gear change. After the intimate scale of the lane house morning — narrow streets, street-level vendors, the compressed domestic architecture of the French Concession — you will walk into a building with a 27-metre internal ceiling height that was, until 2007, generating electricity for this part of Shanghai. The Nanshi Power Plant was built in the 1930s on the west bank of the Huangpu, about five kilometres south of the Bund. It operated for seventy years, survived the Japanese occupation and the Cultural Revolution and the Reform era, and was finally shut down in 2007 when the land was needed for the 2010 World Expo site. After the Expo it was converted into China’s first state-owned contemporary art museum, which opened in 2012. The chimney — 165 metres, still standing — has become part of the Shanghai skyline.

On the 15th Shanghai Biennale “Does the Flower Hear the Bee?” — 250+ works from 67 international artists, examining interspecies communication, plant consciousness, and the porous boundaries between nature and culture. The food logic of a city under pressure

You run a rural buying co-operative in New Zealand. Walking through Shanghai’s wet market this morning, you will be in the presence of something that has no direct equivalent in the NZ context: a food distribution system of genuine complexity serving 26 million people through a combination of centralised state logistics, small-scale private vendors, and informal networks that have evolved over centuries. China has 20% of the world’s population and approximately 7% of its arable land. Food security is not an abstraction for the Chinese government — it is an existential preoccupation that runs through agricultural policy, foreign policy, and the state’s relationship with rural communities in ways that have no Western parallel. The wet market is the downstream end of a system under enormous and ongoing strain. The vendors who have been at their stalls since 4 AM are one node in a supply chain that connects Yangtze Delta farms, Xinjiang lamb operations, East China Sea fisheries, and the Sichuan peppercorn harvest into a single operating system every morning. Pay attention to the logistics. It is remarkable.

What the International Settlement actually was

A brief note on the distinction that matters this morning, because it trips up most visitors: the International Settlement and the French Concession were two separate administrations with different histories, different characters, and different fates. The International Settlement — which covered the Bund and the grid of streets running north and west behind it — was governed by a joint municipal council of foreign powers, initially British and American, eventually joined by most of the industrialised world. It was commercial and institutional: banks, trading houses, newspapers, clubs, consulates. The architecture was designed to communicate power. The Shanghai Club on the Bund had the longest bar in Asia — 34 metres — and a seating arrangement in which the most senior members sat at the end closest to the door and the most junior at the far end. Status in the International Settlement was explicit and hierarchical and encoded in the built environment.

The French Concession, which you’ve been walking through for two days, was different: residential, leafy, administered by French civil law, and chosen by wealthy Chinese families as the place to build their homes precisely because the legal framework offered more security of property than the Chinese-administered city. The Communist Party held its first congress there in 1921 — in a shikumen house in what is now Xintiandi — because the French police were less likely to raid it than the Chinese authorities. Jewish refugees settled in adjacent Hongkou for similar reasons. Shanghai’s foreign concessions were, throughout their existence, places where the normal rules were slightly suspended. This is precisely why so much of the city’s interesting history happened in them.

The Ballard connection In Empire of the Sun, Jim’s family home is in Amherst Avenue — present-day Xinhua Lu — in the French Concession. The walk he takes after his parents disappear covers ground you have already walked: through the settlement, past the Bund, down to the waterfront. Ballard wrote later that he was always fascinated by the way Shanghai could simultaneously contain enormous violence and enormous normality at very close quarters. Walking through the International Settlement’s commercial district this morning, you are in the streets where that coexistence was most pronounced.

Why Pudong is the retort to all of this

One of the most instructive exercises of the morning tour is to listen to the guide describe what the International Settlement was communicating — permanence, Western commercial dominance, the architecture of confident empire — and then think about Pudong. The Chinese government’s decision in 1990 to build a rival financial district on the opposite bank was not primarily economic. It was rhetorical. The buildings that went up in Pudong over the following three decades — taller, more technically ambitious, more immediately iconic than anything on the Bund — were a direct response to the colonial skyline that had defined Shanghai for a century. The Shanghai Tower, completed in 2015 at 632 metres, is the second-tallest building in the world and is, from the Bund, directly aligned with the Customs House. That alignment is not accidental.

From a New Zealand perspective, there is something worth holding in mind as you walk these streets: China is by far New Zealand’s largest export market. The decisions made in the kind of buildings visible from the Bund — and in their Pudong equivalents — ripple directly into the commodity prices that determine Farmlands shareholder returns. This trip is not primarily a business trip, but the city you are walking through is, in a quite direct sense, the city that sets the terms for the sector you work in. That background awareness costs nothing and sharpens everything.

What the French Concession actually was

You have been walking through the French Concession for five days. Today the guide will explain it, and it is worth having the framework in your head before the tour so you can go deeper rather than starting from scratch. The French Concession was not a French colony in any standard sense. It was a zone administered under French civil law, established by treaty in 1849, where the French municipal council had authority but French sovereignty did not technically apply. What this meant in practice was that wealthy Chinese families chose to build their homes here — not in the Chinese-administered city, not in the International Settlement — because French civil law offered more stable property rights than the alternatives. The plane trees were planted by the French Concession municipal council. The shikumen lane houses are a hybrid form invented specifically for this context: European street grid with Chinese domestic courtyard logic, the private interior of the traditional Chinese home adapted to the density of a Western-planned neighbourhood.

The Concession sheltered people the authorities wanted. The Communist Party held multiple congresses in buildings you will walk past. The Nationalist intelligence services operated here because the French police were more amenable to arrangements than the Chinese authorities. Jewish refugees from central Europe in the 1930s lived here. The novelist Zhang Ailing — the great chronicler of Shanghai’s wartime bourgeoisie — wrote here. The French Concession was, throughout its existence, a place where the normal rules were slightly suspended. Its great irony is that it was designed to give Western commerce a foothold in China, and ended up giving Chinese modernity some of its most fertile ground.

What you have been drinking and eating all week

Shaoxing wine (ç»å´é»é) — a golden, slightly sweet, deeply complex amber rice wine aged in clay pots — is the foundational ingredient of Shanghainese cooking. The drunken chicken you had at Jesse: Shaoxing wine. The red-braised pork belly at Chun: Shaoxing wine. The braised fish, the smoked preparations, the slow-cooked everything: Shaoxing wine. You have been eating and drinking Shaoxing for five days without being in it. This is the source. The Jian Lake region around the city has been producing this wine since the Han Dynasty, over 2,000 years ago. The particular combination of the local water chemistry, the local glutinous rice variety, and the wheat qu starter culture that ferments it cannot be replicated anywhere else. Mao Zedong served it at state banquets. It appears in more Tang Dynasty poetry than any other beverage. Zhou Enlai — China’s first Premier under Mao, whose family roots were in Shaoxing — apparently drank it his entire life.

Shaoxing is also where Lu Xun was born. We will come to him properly tomorrow. But it is worth knowing today, arriving into the city, that the writer who effectively invented modern Chinese literature grew up in these streets, along these canals, in the kind of lane house you will walk past this evening. His childhood landscape is embedded in his most important stories in the way that Dublin is embedded in Joyce — not as backdrop but as moral atmosphere. The stinky tofu that Shaoxing is equally famous for — fermented tofu, deep-fried, served with chilli sauce from a street cart — appears in his writing too, as a kind of shorthand for the particular flavour of small-town Chinese life.

The garden and its eight-hundred-year-old love story

Shen’s Garden (æ²å­) is a Song Dynasty garden, roughly 900 years old. Go before 9:30 AM. After that, the tour groups arrive. What makes the garden worth the early hour is not its beauty — it is modest, deliberately understated, smaller than Suzhou’s classical gardens — but its story. The poet Lu You (éæ¸¸, 1125–1210) married his cousin Tang Wan. His mother, disapproving, forced the marriage to end. Both remarried. Years later — years — they happened to encounter each other in this garden. Lu You was overwhelmed. He wrote a poem on the garden wall. Tang Wan read it, and wrote a response. Both poems — “Phoenix Hairpin” (éå¤´å¤) — have been memorised by Chinese schoolchildren for eight hundred years. They are on the wall in the garden now. In Chinese culture, which values emotional restraint to a degree that Western culture does not, the two Phoenix Hairpin poems are among the most nakedly raw expressions of grief and longing in the literary canon. They are also, in the context of a Song Dynasty garden on a spring morning in Shaoxing, extraordinarily moving. The replica inscription is not the original. Go and find the weight in it anyway.

Lu Xun and why he still matters

Lu Xun (é²è¿, born Zhou Shuren, 1881–1936) is the most important writer in modern Chinese literature and, by the reckoning of most scholars who have worked in both traditions, one of the most significant writers of the twentieth century by any standard. He grew up in Shaoxing in a declining scholar-gentry family — his grandfather was imprisoned for bribery when Lu Xun was thirteen, his father was dying of tuberculosis, the family wealth was dissipating. He went to Japan to study medicine. The story of what changed his direction is one of the most famous conversion narratives in Chinese cultural history: he was watching a lantern-slide presentation about the Russo-Japanese War when an image appeared of a Chinese man about to be beheaded by Japanese soldiers while a crowd of Chinese onlookers watched impassively. The expressions on the faces of the crowd — not horrified, not resistant, simply watching — struck him as the central problem of China. He dropped medicine and became a writer. The question his entire body of work addresses is: why does a society acquiesce in its own destruction?

His most famous stories — “A Madman’s Diary” (1918), “The True Story of Ah Q” (1921), “Kong Yiji” (1919) — are savage examinations of Chinese social conformity, the failure of Confucian tradition to produce moral individuals rather than compliant ones, and what he called, in his most quoted passage, the “cannibalism” of Chinese society: the way in which the social order devoured the people within it. He remains genuinely subversive and is simultaneously held up as a national hero by the Chinese government, which is the kind of contradiction that China manages with particular fluency. He is taught in every Chinese school. The edgiest parts of his critique are quietly de-emphasised.

On “Kong Yiji” and lunch at Xianheng “Kong Yiji” is a short story published in 1919. It takes place in the Xianheng Tavern — the restaurant where you are having lunch — and is narrated by a young bar assistant who describes the periodic appearances of Kong Yiji, a failed examination scholar who wears the long gown of the educated class despite being destitute, drinks rice wine on credit, and is mocked by the other customers for pretending to an education he cannot use. The story ends with Kong Yiji arriving one day, apparently having had his legs broken as punishment for theft, crawling on his hands. He drinks his last bowl of wine and is never seen again. The other customers do not intervene. The narrator does not intervene. The cruelty is not active; it is the cruelty of an audience that finds amusement in watching someone fail. Lu Xun was describing the examination system that had produced Kong Yiji — that produced thousands of Kong Yijis, men educated in the classics for the sole purpose of passing exams that most of them would fail — and the social indifference that finished them off. In March 2023, Chinese youth who couldn’t find employment despite their university degrees started calling themselves Kong Yijis. The story is still active.

Order the fennel broad beans (è´é¦è±) and warm Huadiao wine. These are the exact items Kong Yiji orders at the bar in the story. The dish, eaten standing at the bar, is the correct way to do it. If the tables are full, stand. One bowl of wine, one dish of beans. You will have read the story, you will know the garden, you will have walked the street where Lu Xun grew up. Eat slowly.

The week, tied together

Tomorrow at 5:45 AM you leave this hotel and begin the journey home via Shanghai. It is worth spending a few minutes this evening with the week just completed. You arrived in Shanghai — a city whose skyline did not exist forty years ago, built on the site of the world’s most cosmopolitan port city, which in turn was built on a minor cotton-trading village — and you spent five days watching how a society operates when it is moving at full speed after a long stall. You ate in the wet markets. You stood in the former power plant. You walked the streets where the Communist Party held its first meetings in borrowed rooms. You watched Qingming: the dead receiving their paper iPhones. You went to a slaughterhouse designed by a British architect with Art Deco motifs on the windows to help the cattle reincarnate.

And now you are in Shaoxing, which has been here for 2,500 years and which produced the rice wine that underpins all that Shanghai food, and the writer who diagnosed China’s central pathology a century ago, and the canal system that the entire Yangtze Delta was organised around before the railways came. The contrast between Shanghai and Shaoxing is not the contrast between modernity and tradition. It is the contrast between two kinds of persistence: the city that burned everything and rebuilt at speed, and the city that simply stayed.

Ballard’s Jim, in the final pages of Empire of the Sun, returns to Shanghai after the war and walks through the empty rooms of the Amherst Avenue house. Everything that defined his childhood — the furniture, the servants, the cocktail parties, the entire social world — is gone. The city is the same. The buildings are intact. Only the people who thought they owned it have disappeared. Shanghai absorbed them, as it absorbed everything. It is still absorbing. Pay attention to what it is absorbing now.
